# GTM Source Map

Use this reference to find good "flesh" for GTM decisions. Separate method sources, market context, and direct customer evidence.

## Source Classes

### 1. User Evidence

Best for actual recommendations:

- Customer interviews
- Sales calls and demos
- CRM notes
- Support tickets
- Waitlist signup reasons
- Community conversations
- Product usage analytics
- Retention and activation data
- Revenue, renewal, expansion, and churn data
- Customer quotes and testimonials

Use for:

- Pain intensity
- Urgency
- Current alternatives
- Willingness to pay
- Buyer role
- Positioning language
- Proof needs

### 2. Competitor and Alternative Evidence

Useful sources:

- Competitor homepages
- Pricing pages
- Docs, onboarding, changelogs
- Case studies
- Comparison pages
- Review sites such as G2, Capterra, TrustRadius
- Product Hunt, AppSumo, Chrome Web Store, Shopify App Store, Atlassian Marketplace, GitHub Marketplace

Use for:

- Alternative mapping
- Category language
- Differentiation hypotheses
- Switching cost clues
- Pricing expectations
- Common objections

Do not use competitor claims as proof that the user's product can win.

### 3. Buyer and Pain Language

Useful sources:

- Reddit
- Hacker News
- LinkedIn posts and comments
- Niche Slack or Discord communities
- GitHub issues and discussions for developer tools
- Stack Overflow and technical forums
- Industry forums
- Podcast transcripts and webinars
- Conference agendas and session descriptions

Use for:

- Pain wording
- Trigger events
- Buying anxieties
- Current workarounds
- Community reachability

Treat anonymous comments as directional, not decisive.

### 4. Demand and Discovery Signals

Useful sources:

- Google Trends
- Keyword tools
- Search results and People Also Ask
- SEO tools such as Ahrefs, Semrush, Similarweb
- Marketplace search behavior
- Product Hunt and launch directories

Use for:

- Search-led or content-led feasibility
- Category awareness
- Existing demand versus education burden

Do not equate search volume with willingness to pay.

### 5. Company and Category Signals

Useful sources:

- LinkedIn company pages and job posts
- Crunchbase, PitchBook, CB Insights, Dealroom, Tracxn
- Funding announcements
- Analyst reports
- Public company annual reports
- Vendor ecosystem maps

Use for:

- Market maturity
- Budget owner clues
- Category momentum
- Competitive density
- Partner ecosystem clues

Treat as context, not customer evidence.

### 6. Method Sources

Use for frameworks and language:

- Y Combinator Startup Library: customer discovery, founder sales, MVP validation
- First Round Review: founder-led sales, positioning, early GTM cases
- Reforge: growth loops, activation, retention, channels
- Lenny's Newsletter: SaaS, marketplace, PLG, pricing, growth cases
- Product Marketing Alliance: PMM workflows, launch, positioning, enablement
- April Dunford: positioning and competitive alternatives
- Crossing the Chasm: beachhead and whole product
- Jobs to Be Done: trigger, struggling moment, current workaround
- Lean Startup and customer development: hypothesis and learning loop
- OpenView, Bessemer, a16z, Sequoia, NFX, Redpoint: SaaS, PLG, category and market-entry essays

Use these as reasoning scaffolds. Do not cite them as evidence about the user's market.

## Research Routing

Choose sources based on the decision:

| Decision needed | Best sources | Weak sources |
|---|---|---|
| Beachhead segment | Customer interviews, sales notes, CRM, community pain language | TAM reports, broad trend essays |
| GTM motion | Price, ACV, buyer role, product complexity, activation data, sales notes | Competitor channel guesses |
| Positioning | Interview language, alternatives, win/loss, reviews | Founder adjectives, generic category copy |
| Channel bet | Buyer reachability, community activity, search behavior, existing audience | Channel popularity |
| Willingness to pay | Pricing conversations, signed deals, budget owner evidence | Company size alone |
| Urgency | Trigger events, deadlines, failed workarounds, active search | Problem severity in theory |

## Research Output Standard

When using external sources, summarize:

- Source type
- What it suggests
- Whether it is strong, medium, or weak evidence
- Which decision it affects
- What still needs customer validation

Do not bury weak evidence inside confident recommendations.
