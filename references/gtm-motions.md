# GTM Motions

Use this reference to choose a primary go-to-market motion and identify when a popular motion is a poor fit.

## Motion Selection Factors

Evaluate:

- Buyer type: user, manager, executive, procurement, developer, consumer
- Price point and ACV
- Product complexity and setup burden
- Trust, security, compliance, or data sensitivity
- Time to value
- Proof burden
- Existing audience or distribution access
- Sales cycle length
- Whether the buyer already searches for the category

## Motion Types

### Founder-Led Sales

Use when:

- Product is early and learning matters more than scale.
- Buyer pain and objections are still unclear.
- ACV justifies conversations.
- Trust must be built manually.

Avoid when:

- Price is too low to justify calls.
- User needs instant self-serve experience.

First action:

- Run 10 to 20 targeted conversations with the most specific beachhead buyers.

### Product-Led Growth

Use when:

- User can reach value quickly without heavy setup.
- Product has clear self-serve activation.
- The user and buyer are close or the user can create bottom-up pull.
- Pricing can start low and expand.

Avoid when:

- Procurement, security, migration, or executive approval blocks adoption.
- Value appears only after team-wide rollout or long data accumulation.

First action:

- Define activation event and test whether new users reach it without help.

### Outbound-Led

Use when:

- Buyer role and trigger can be clearly identified.
- Pain is acute enough to interrupt someone.
- Offer can be explained in a short message.
- ACV supports manual acquisition.

Avoid when:

- Buyer is vague.
- Pain is educational, latent, or nice-to-have.
- The team cannot build relevant lists.

First action:

- Build a 50-account list around one trigger and test reply quality, not just open rate.

### Content-Led

Use when:

- Buyers search, compare, or learn before purchasing.
- The team has credible expertise or data.
- Category education is needed.
- Time horizon allows compounding.

Avoid when:

- The team needs immediate revenue.
- The topic has no search or community behavior.
- Content would be generic listicles.

First action:

- Publish 3 to 5 high-intent assets tied to buyer pain and track qualified conversations.

### Community-Led

Use when:

- Buyers or users gather in identifiable communities.
- Trust and peer learning drive adoption.
- The product benefits from champions or shared practice.

Avoid when:

- The target buyers are not community-active.
- The team intends to extract demand without contributing.

First action:

- Identify 3 communities and test whether the pain language appears organically.

### Developer-Led

Use when:

- Developers are users or champions.
- Docs, API, SDK, GitHub, examples, and technical proof matter.
- Adoption can start with an individual developer.

Avoid when:

- Developers are not empowered to adopt or influence.
- Product value is mostly executive or financial.

First action:

- Test docs/demo value with 5 to 10 target developers and measure completion to first useful result.

### Partner or Channel-Led

Use when:

- Buyers already trust intermediaries.
- The product complements agencies, consultants, platforms, or integrators.
- Direct access is expensive or slow.

Avoid when:

- The product is not packaged enough for partners to explain.
- Margin, enablement, or support model is unclear.

First action:

- Interview 5 potential partners before building partner materials.

### Marketplace-Led

Use when:

- The buyer already searches inside a marketplace.
- The product integrates with a platform ecosystem.
- Reviews, listing quality, and platform trust can drive discovery.

Avoid when:

- The marketplace is crowded and differentiation is unclear.
- The product requires education outside the platform.

First action:

- Audit top marketplace competitors and test listing-message clarity with target users.
