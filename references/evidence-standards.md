# Evidence Standards

Use this reference to judge whether a GTM recommendation is supported, assumed, or risky.

## Evidence Hierarchy

| Strength | Evidence type | How to use it |
|---|---|---|
| Strong | Paying customers, signed contracts, renewal behavior, expansion, repeated sales patterns | Can support high-confidence segment, motion, and pain claims |
| Strong | Sales call notes, win/loss notes, demo transcripts, buyer objections repeated across calls | Can support pain, urgency, alternative, and messaging claims |
| Medium | Active waitlist with stated reasons, high-intent inbound, repeated community requests | Can support validation direction, not final motion |
| Medium | Product usage data, activation behavior, retention patterns | Can support user value, but may not reveal buyer or willingness to pay |
| Medium | Support tickets, feature requests, customer quotes | Can support pain language and proof gaps |
| Weak | Competitor websites, pricing pages, reviews, public case studies | Useful for alternatives and market context, not user-specific proof |
| Weak | Search volume, trend data, analyst reports, funding news | Useful for demand context, not beachhead urgency |
| Weak | Founder intuition, generic personas, broad TAM | Treat as assumptions |

## Confidence Labels

Use three labels:

- High confidence: multiple strong signals point to the same segment, pain, and willingness to pay.
- Medium confidence: some direct evidence exists, but key parts rely on inference.
- Low confidence: mostly public data, founder belief, or untested assumptions.

Never use high confidence if no user/customer evidence exists.

## Required Assumption Labels

Mark a claim as an assumption when:

- The buyer has not been directly observed or interviewed.
- Willingness to pay is inferred from company size or role rather than actual price discussion.
- Urgency is inferred from problem severity but no one has tried to solve it.
- Channel reachability is inferred from platform popularity.
- Competitor weakness is inferred without win/loss or customer switching evidence.
- Positioning depends on a proof point the user does not yet have.

## Red Flags

Flag these explicitly:

- Big market, vague buyer.
- Broad ICP such as "SMBs", "creators", "developers", or "enterprise" without use case or trigger.
- Strong product feature but no buying event.
- User wants PLG but the product needs trust, setup, data migration, procurement, or executive approval.
- User wants outbound but cannot identify a buyer role and trigger.
- User wants content-led growth but cannot sustain topic authority or publishing quality.
- User wants enterprise motion without references, security posture, implementation support, or sales capacity.
- User chooses a channel because competitors use it, not because the buyer is reachable there.
- User treats signups as demand without activation, usage, or payment evidence.

## Evidence Ledger Format

Use this table in final briefs:

| Decision | Evidence | Assumption | Risk if wrong | Validation action |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| [Recommendation] | [Direct support] | [Inferred part] | [Consequence] | [Next test] |

## Source Guidance

Use public sources for market context:

- Competitor sites, docs, pricing, changelogs, case studies
- G2, Capterra, TrustRadius, Product Hunt, AppSumo
- Reddit, Hacker News, Discord or Slack communities, niche forums
- LinkedIn company pages, job posts, buyer profiles
- Google Trends and keyword tools
- Crunchbase, PitchBook, CB Insights, Dealroom, Tracxn

Use user-provided sources for actual decisions:

- Customer interviews
- Sales calls and demos
- CRM notes
- Waitlist signup reasons
- Product analytics
- Revenue and retention
- Support tickets
- Community conversations
- Customer quotes
